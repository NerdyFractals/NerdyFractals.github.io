Start here

How to automate your first flight

You don't need to write a flight controller from scratch. The ecosystem already exists — your job is to learn the layers and glue them together.

1

Pick a flight stack

ArduPilot or PX4 both run on cheap hardware and speak MAVLink. ArduPilot has a larger hobbyist community; PX4 is common in research.

2

Simulate before you fly

SITL (Software In The Loop) lets you run ArduPilot inside Gazebo or Mission Planner on your laptop — crash virtually, learn fast.

3

Talk to it with DroneKit or MAVSDK

Python libraries that wrap MAVLink. A ten-line script can arm, take off, fly a waypoint mission, and land.

4

Add a companion computer

A Raspberry Pi or Jetson Nano sits on the drone and runs your vision pipeline. It sends velocity or position commands to the flight controller over a serial or USB MAVLink link.

5

Close the loop with a camera

OpenCV + AprilTags (or YOLO for object detection) gives the drone a reference frame. Now it can see and react — precision landing, follow-me, obstacle avoidance.

The landscape

Where drones are reshaping industries

Delivery networks (Wing, Zipline, Amazon), agricultural spraying and surveying, infrastructure inspection (power lines, wind turbines, bridges), construction progress tracking, cinematography, search and rescue — the list gets longer every quarter.

The common thread: tasks that are dull, dirty, or dangerous for humans become cheap, repeatable, and data-rich when handed to an autonomous system. The bottleneck is no longer the hardware — it's software, regulation, and the humans who understand both.

That's the gap this community fills. Pilots who can write code. Developers who can fly. People who understand the full stack from firmware to web dashboard to FAA Part 107.
